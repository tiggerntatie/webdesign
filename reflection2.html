Web Design Reflection 2: Think About Navigation

Most web sites that consist of more than a single page have a home page (like your index.html) that visitors see first. A good home page should help your visitors find the information that they want as quickly as possible. To accomplish this, most home pages have some kind of navigation design that helps visitors access any page in the site with relatively few clicks.

Please visit again your most and least favorite web sites and look at how the site navigation design works. Find and visit a THIRD site that has an unusual navigation design (you may want Google to help you with this). Web designers LOVE to collect unusual ideas for site navigation. Answer these questions about each of the three sites:

  1. Does the site have a navigation system?
  2. Describe the location of the navigation bar or menu. At the top, bottom, sprawled across the middle are possible answers.
  3. How does the navigation system work? What does the user have to do to find a particular page at the site?
  4. Is the navigation system easy to understand and use? Please explain why!

For example:

The Hanover High web site has a navigation system. The navigation system is a vertical list of links at the left hand side of the page. To use the system, hover the mouse over an item and wait for a sub-menu of clickable links will pop up over the page. I find these menus easy to understand but difficult to navigate because if my mouse cursor slips off the original item as I move to the sub-menu, then a different sub-menu might be displayed instead. It usually takes me two or three tries to navigate where I want to go.
